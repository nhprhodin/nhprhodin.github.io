Research Interests

Welcome to my research page!

I am an observer at heart, and spend most of my time extracting information encoded in astronomical images taken with world-class ground- and space-based telescopes. Simply put, I explore to what degree of confidence we can make statements on galaxy formation and evolution based on such observations. This is no easy task, and admittedly my scientific contribution to this feat plays a marginal role, if any.

I tackle this challenge by posing a rather convoluted question: how sensitive are the conclusions reached to the observations they are based on? Innocent-looking, this question carries depth and touches on the foundations of the scientific method. Rather than the standard (controlled) experiments, astronomy is an observational science, confined to the light that reaches us. But what we lack in experimental control, we can make up for in numbers. With the grandness of the cosmos at our disposal, we therefore leverage our results with statistics. As a part of this, we must control for our selection-functions - i.e. how we select the objects which we observe and base our conclusions on. That is where I come in.

Phrased in the astronomer's "lingo", my current research focuses on galaxies selected via their gas cross section towards background quasars. This technique avoids bias towards the most luminous “tip of the ice-berg” accessed by conventional methods (luminosity selected samples), and is believed to probe a more representative portion of the gas-rich galaxy population. In particular, I want to understand the conditions for star formation; the metal-enrichment of the circum-galactic medium; and which sight-lines through galaxies cause damped neutral Hydrogen (HI) absorption (Damped Lyman-α absorption (DLAs) and Sub-DLAs).
